You’re right — I’m going to treat **Role on Team** with a stricter NBA front-office lens now.

The corrected standard:

Role on Team is not “how much usage does he have?”  
Role on Team is not “how famous is he?”  
Role on Team is not “is he the best offensive player on a bad team?”

Role on Team \= how the player is actually viewed/deployed in the team ecosystem:  
rotation burden, organizational trust, stability, leverage, contract/team-building status, and whether the team is genuinely built around him.

That means a player can be:

Offensive Role: Primary Offensive Engine  
Role on Team: Heavy-Minute Starter

That is exactly the Trae example. He can still run a ton of possessions, but he is not automatically a **Franchise Cornerstone** anymore. He is now a high-usage offensive engine whose team-role status should be treated with shrinkage and skepticism, not with cornerstone certainty. For current-team context, he is now with Washington, not Atlanta. ([Washington Wizards](https://washingtonwizards.com/news/wizards-acquire-four-time-all-star-trae-young?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

This correction matters because role-on-team directly affects the proof-profile shrinkage layer.

A **Franchise Cornerstone** gets:

higher responsibility prior  
team-validation burden  
less excuse for mediocre team outcomes  
more COV/IIB accountability  
larger effective sample if playing big minutes

A **Heavy-Minute Starter** gets:

starter-level sample trust  
but less organizational/team-building burden than FC  
less aggressive team-outcome blame than FC

A **Sixth Man / Impact Sub** gets:

bench-context discount  
starter-validation check  
star-overlap check  
closing-lineup validation

A **Tactical Starter** gets:

matchup/context shrinkage  
closing-trust check  
do-not-overrate-starting-label rule

That is why Trae matters as a test case. His offensive role can still be:

Primary Offensive Engine

but his role-on-team should be:

Heavy-Minute Starter

and his proof profile should trigger:

High-usage creation hub with team-validation shrinkage

That is the right architecture.

This assessment should be treated as a **2025-26 role-on-team pass**, not a 6-year average and not a 2026-27 projection.

The lens should be:

2025-26 observed/current team context  
not career reputation  
not peak version  
not contract size alone  
not usage alone  
not “who was he three years ago?”

So the rules are:

Franchise Cornerstone \= true 2025-26 franchise/team-building axis

Heavy-Minute Starter \= major starter/high-trust player, but not necessarily the franchise axis

Tactical Starter \= starts or plays starter-level role because of fit/matchup/context, but not guaranteed as a universal closing or team-building pillar

Sixth Man / Impact Sub \= real bench engine / high-impact reserve

Core Rotational Player \= stable rotation piece

Situational Specialist \= narrow skill or matchup-use player

Energy / Spark Plug \= short-burst momentum/pressure player

Garbage Time / Active DNP / Inactive \= actual low/no-rotation status

The important correction from earlier is exactly what you caught:

Primary offensive engine ≠ Franchise Cornerstone

So for **2025-26**, someone like Trae can still be:

Offensive Role: Primary Offensive Engine  
Proof Profile: High-usage creation hub with team-validation shrinkage  
Role on Team: Heavy-Minute Starter

not Franchise Cornerstone.

Also, any rookies/prospects who are not actually in a 2025-26 observed NBA role should stay **Needs Review** unless you explicitly want a separate **Projected 2026-27 Role on Team** field.
